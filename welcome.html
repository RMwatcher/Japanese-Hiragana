Hiragana basics

Hiragana is used for native Japanese in their writing. Hiragana consists of:

  • 46 Basic Hiragana Syllables
  • 23 Additional Sounds (voiced Consonants)
  • 33 Additional Sounds (contracted sounds)
  • Double Consonants
  • Long Vowels

For Hiragana and Katakana:

  1. Two syllabaries, or a single syllable sounds, that represent a same sets of phonetic sounds.
  2. Learning both Hiragana and Katakana will teach you all the distinct sounds of Japanese.
  3. They're both fairly quick and easy to learn.
  4. Each character can only have one pronunciation. No two characters can sound alike.
  5. Each Hiragana characters don't have meaning attached to them.

We would learn more about Katakana at a later time, but just remember that they share some characteristics with Hiragana but serve a different purpose to the Japanese writing system. As a general note, Japanese does not use spaces to separate the words.

Two-Dashes and small circle Hiragana

As mentioned earlier, some of these characters can go into additional sounds simply by adding two small dashes or a tiny circle as shown below.

a i u e o
あ い う え お
g が ぎ ぐ げ ご
z ざ じ ず ぜ ぞ
d だ ぢ づ で ど
b ば び ぶ べ ぼ
p ぱ ぴ ぷ ぺ ぽ

As you can see, the characters of:

  • k becomes g
  • s becomes z
  • t becomes d
  • h becomes b
  • h also becomes p

The syllables look the same except it has a additional symbol at the top right of each character to tell them apart. They also produce a different sound compare to the basic syllables.
